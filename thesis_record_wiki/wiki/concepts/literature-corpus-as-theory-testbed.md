# Summary

The lit-review literature corpus operated as a theory-to-schema testbed. Its value was not just collecting readings; it exposed whether one extraction and schema-generation method could handle very different theory forms across social science, political science, communication, behavior change, intervention design, and information operations.

# Why It Matters

The corpus breadth explains several later findings:

- property-graph defaults looked attractive for some sources but failed as a universal representation
- multi-theory and hybrid papers created routing and decomposition problems
- sequential theories, taxonomies, statistical results, intervention frameworks, and argumentation ontologies needed different schema forms
- prompt calibration and specialized model detection became necessary because the source distribution was intentionally heterogeneous

This connects the early annotated bibliography and old-schema corpus to later validation problems rather than treating them as unrelated artifacts.

# Remaining-Folder Evidence

[Lit Review Remaining Literature Folders](/wiki/sources/lit-review-remaining-literature-folders.md) shows that many preserved folders are text-only, while the remaining generated schemas outside the already-ingested deep slices are poliheuristic decision analysis and Chong/Druckman framing theory. That supports a staged ingest strategy: inventory the text-only corpus, then deep-dive generated schemas and code-like artifacts first. [1][2]
