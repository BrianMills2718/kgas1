# Summary

The proposal variants show an evolution from an expansive theory-automation architecture toward a dissertation-safe proof-of-concept proposal. That change should not be read as "newer is better." It is a change in rhetorical and evidentiary function.

The expansive version preserves the thesis ambition: KGAS as infrastructure for automated theory operationalization, cross-modal analysis, theory-to-workflow generation, and future LLM-driven social-science research. [1]

The critique and final-submission variants preserve the academic constraint: the proposal should frame KGAS as proposed feasibility work, baseline establishment, methodological infrastructure, and construct-estimate validation rather than as already functional production software. [2][3][4]

The durable reading is therefore a layered one: older expansive files are valuable for intellectual scope and architecture; later rewrite files are valuable for dissertation tone, page constraints, validation discipline, and committee-facing risk control.

# Main Delta

The central delta is claim discipline, not idea replacement.

The older architecture guide says KGAS is designed to demonstrate automated theory operationalization: literature becomes theory schemas, schemas become executable analysis specifications, and workflows switch across graph/table/vector representations according to theoretical requirements. [1]

The writing guidance narrows that into dissertation-safe claims: use future tense for proposed work, present tense only for existing facts, frame theory names as examples, describe "construct estimates" rather than stronger measurement claims, and treat the system as proof-of-concept infrastructure rather than a production system. [2]

The critique file then hardens the boundary: do not invent validation approaches or results, remove production-ready/functionality language, center COVID construct-estimate validation, and simplify claims around tool counts, SME validation, universal-platform language, and UFO material. [3]

The final revision layer shows the edits made under that discipline: "lossless" became "information-preserving conversions between formats within theoretical constructs of interest"; RQ wording shifted from "improve" to "characterize improvements"; RQ2 became documentation rather than optimization; and operational indicators for "insight" were defined. [7]

# What Got Better, Worse, Or Merely Different

**Better for committee submission:** The later proposal materials are stronger as academic artifacts because they reduce overclaiming, foreground validation, and separate main-text scope from annex detail. [6][7]

**Potentially worse for intellectual recovery:** The compressed final proposal necessarily hides or moves material. The older expansive architecture and archived fragments preserve ideas that matter for reconstructing the system's evolution: dynamic tool generation, schema discovery, full examples, uncertainty propagation, and tool orchestration. [1][8]

**Not directly comparable:** The final-submission summary is not a complete replacement for older proposal drafts. It is an index and readiness report. Older full drafts and fragments may still preserve rationale, transitions, and abandoned alternatives not visible in the summary. [6][8]

**Unresolved tension:** The architecture guide's automation framing is stronger than the critique file's strict "designed to" proposal framing. This is not a contradiction to erase. It marks the boundary between system vision and dissertation claim posture. [1][3]

# Archive-Disposition Caveat

The 2025 systematic assessment labels `proposal_old/`, fragments, supporting research materials, and duplicate schema files as archive candidates. [8] Under the current thesis-record policy, that does not mean they should be deleted or ignored.

For Brian's current goal, "archive candidate" means:

- likely not the first place to extract reusable current architecture;
- still valuable as provenance;
- useful for reconstructing why scope, terminology, and validation language changed;
- should remain preserved and cited when comparing variants.

This is especially important because older fragments can explain why later proposals softened claims. A cleanup-oriented assessment and a provenance-oriented review answer different questions.

# Practical Review Rule

When reviewing proposal variants, classify each difference into one of five buckets:

1. **Claim softening**: a stronger claim was intentionally weakened for academic integrity.
2. **Scope compression**: material moved to annexes or archives because of page constraints.
3. **Validation substitution**: broad success criteria were replaced by feasible tests.
4. **Terminology stabilization**: language changed to match dissertation framing.
5. **Idea preservation**: older material remains valuable even if not present in the final proposal.

Do not collapse these buckets into a linear "old bad, new good" story.
