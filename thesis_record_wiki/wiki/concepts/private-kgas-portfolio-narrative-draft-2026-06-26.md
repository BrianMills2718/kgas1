# Making GraphRAG Theory-Aware

KGAS was my attempt to make GraphRAG theory-aware.

Most retrieval systems are good at finding relevant text. Social-science research needs a different layer on top of retrieval: theories, constructs, operational definitions, evidence quality, and explicit uncertainty. KGAS explored what it would take to make those research objects executable inside an AI-assisted analysis system.

The core idea was a pipeline:

```text
paper -> theory schema -> model-form routing -> discourse application -> comparable outputs -> validation evidence
```

Instead of treating a theory as background reading for an analyst, KGAS treated a theory as an operational artifact. A paper could be analyzed for its vocabulary, constructs, formal notation, algorithms, assumptions, and application rules. Those pieces could then become a schema that guided downstream analysis. Some theories wanted graphs. Others wanted sequences, tables, statistical models, hypergraphs, or hybrid representations. The point was not to force everything into one graph shape; it was to choose a representation that matched the theory.

This work grew from the GraphRAG/DIGIMON lineage, which decomposes graph-based retrieval into methods, graph types, and retrieval operators. KGAS extended that frame toward computational social science: if GraphRAG can organize documents and retrieval operators, then a theory-aware version should also organize constructs, model forms, evidence claims, and validation boundaries.

The most important result is not a finished product claim. The valuable result is a preserved research lineage showing what theory-aware analysis infrastructure requires:

- source vocabulary must be preserved, not summarized away;
- formal notation and algorithms are part of theory content;
- schema variants can represent different valid operational interpretations;
- model-form routing matters because theories are not all graphs;
- generated outputs need evidence labels, not just fluent explanations;
- current runtime proof, historical evidence, proposal claims, and future validation plans must stay separate.

The uncertainty work sharpened that lesson. KGAS moved through normalized confidence scores, qualitative evidence frameworks, entity-resolution uncertainty, Bayesian and IC-inspired experiments, local construct-mapping guidance, and current-code verification. The safest synthesis is not that KGAS discovered one final uncertainty formula. It is that research systems need auditable uncertainty reasoning tied to the evidence actually available.

That evidence discipline is why the private archive matters. The record contains working paths, failed paths, superseded designs, old ambitions, runtime repairs, validation plans, and claim corrections. Keeping that history is useful because research infrastructure evolves through those tensions. But the raw archive is not a public artifact. It may contain logs, local configuration, sensitive historical material, and data that requires separate review. The right public-facing artifact should be derived from the archive, not be the archive itself.

If I continued this work, I would build a smaller theory-schema workbench: import a public paper, extract a theory schema, apply it to public text, compare graph/table/vector or hybrid representations, and report evidence and uncertainty boundaries without overstating validation. The goal would be modest but concrete: show how social-science theory can become executable enough to support analysis while staying honest about what has and has not been proven.
