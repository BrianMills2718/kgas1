# Summary

The final proposal annexes are not secondary clutter. They are where the compressed final proposal preserves much of the technical and governance content that earlier long-form drafts carried in the main text.

| Annex | Lines | Preservation value |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| Annex A: Theory Meta-Schema | 252 | Full schema architecture, methodology types, Social Identity Theory example, cross-theory integration, and QA process. [1] |
| Annex B: Technical Architecture | 308 | Tool suites, DAG orchestration, Neo4j/SQLite bi-store, processing stages, low-code UI, MCP/external integration, and scaling strategies. [2] |
| Annex C: Validation Framework | 268 | Fourteen uncertainty dimensions, Davis validity framework, multi-level validation, theory-specific validation patterns, uncertainty propagation math, and evidence requirements. [3] |
| Annex D: HSPC | 212 | Human-subjects boundary, data protection, SME consultation triggers, LLM ethics, and modification/oversight process. [4] |
| Annex E: Timeline | 344 | Month-by-month essay schedule, milestones, risks, resources, and defense path. [5] |
| Annex F: Glossary | 106 | Stabilized terminology for KGAS, construct estimate, cross-modal analysis, validation without optimization, uncertainty, and policy-analysis terms. [6] |

The important preservation conclusion is that the final proposal's shorter main text should not be mistaken for intellectual deletion. Many technical details were moved to annexes, where they remain recoverable and citable.

# Annex A - Theory Meta-Schema

Annex A preserves the core theory-to-computation schema design. It defines metadata, theoretical structure, computational representation, and algorithms as the major components of a machine-readable social-science theory. [1]

It also keeps the methodology-type vocabulary: formulas, algorithms, procedures, rules, and sequences. This matters because it shows that "theory" was not being reduced to a property graph alone. The proposal expected theory content to include mathematical equations, graph algorithms, conditional rules, procedural steps, and temporal stages. [1]

The Social Identity Theory JSON example is especially valuable because it is a concrete target format: entities, relations, modifiers, graph/table/vector specs, formulas, logical rules, procedural steps, and validation expectations. [1]

# Annex B - Technical Architecture

Annex B preserves the proposal's technical architecture after main-text compression. It lists document-processing, graph, statistical, vector, cross-modal converter, and agent-based modeling tool suites. [2]

It also gives a DAG orchestration sketch with node/edge/state concepts, automatic DAG generation steps, and execution patterns. This preserves the intended agent-drivable workflow model: parse a question, match theory, select operations, resolve dependencies, and configure parameters. [2]

The storage section records the Neo4j + SQLite bi-store rationale. Neo4j is for graph entities, relationships, paths, and embeddings; SQLite is for workflow execution, provenance, outputs, and operational metadata. [2]

The processing section describes four stages: document ingestion, theory-guided extraction, cross-modal analysis, and result synthesis. Treat this as target architecture and proposal intent, not evidence that every stage was live at proposal time. [2]

# Annex C - Validation Framework

Annex C is the strongest validation-preservation artifact in the final package. It names fourteen uncertainty dimensions, including source credibility, coherence, temporal relevance, extraction completeness, entity recognition, construct validity, theory-data fit, study limitations, sampling bias, diagnosticity, sufficiency, calibration, entity resolution, and reasoning-chain validity. [3]

It also maps KGAS validation onto Davis's five-dimensional framework: description, causal explanation, postdiction, exploratory validity, and prediction. Prediction is explicitly marked aspirational. [3]

The uncertainty-propagation worked example combines entity extraction, network construction, and centrality uncertainty using root-sum-squares, then translates the interval into IC-style probability language. [3]

The most important claim-discipline section is C.6. It says each validation claim requires metric definition, test procedure, results, evidence files, and limitations. It also states the success philosophy: no predetermined targets, baseline establishment, transparent reporting, and reproducibility. [3]

# Annex D - HSPC And Ethics Boundary

Annex D records the human-subjects boundary. It frames the dissertation as not human subjects research under the current design because it uses public/de-identified data, no intervention, no private identifiable information, and no direct individual interaction. [4]

It still preserves modification triggers: systematic SME evaluation, restricted datasets, external participants, identifiable information, or funding/IRB requirements would require HSPC notification or review. [4]

This is important because older proposal material used SME-centered language heavily. Annex D shows how the final proposal contained that risk: informal RAND staff consultation may occur, but systematic SME evaluation becomes a review-triggering change. [4]

# Annex E - Timeline

Annex E preserves a much longer schedule than the compressed main proposal. It lays out proposal submission in August 2025, Essay 1 from September-November 2025, Essay 2 from December 2025-March 2026, Essay 3 from April-June 2026, integration from July-September 2026, and defense preparation through February 2027. [5]

This is a provenance caveat because another final rewrite section says "12 months from proposal approval (September 2025) through defense (February 2026)," which is internally inconsistent with the annex's 18-month September 2025-February 2027 schedule. See [Proposal Old To Final Comparison 2026 06 26](/wiki/sources/proposal-old-to-final-comparison-2026-06-26.md).

The annex likely preserves the fuller planning intent; the main-text compressed timeline may preserve an editing inconsistency or unresolved schedule change. Do not silently reconcile these.

The timeline-specific review records the full discrepancy: older proposal versions targeted February 2026, the full August draft contains both 6-month and 12-month language, and the final rewrite plus Annex E target February 2027. See [Proposal Timeline Chronology Discrepancy 2026 06 26](/wiki/sources/proposal-timeline-chronology-discrepancy-2026-06-26.md).

# Annex F - Glossary

Annex F preserves stabilized vocabulary. It defines "construct estimate" as a computational approximation, not perfect measurement; "information-preserving" as preserving theoretical constructs of interest without claiming losslessness; and "validation without optimization" as documenting actual performance rather than tuning for benchmarks. [6]

This glossary is useful as a dictionary for reading the final proposal. It also helps identify why earlier terminology changed: "computational proxy" and "lossless" language were being replaced by terms that better match claim discipline. [6]

# Main Preservation Lesson

For future thesis recovery, do not compare only main proposal drafts. The final submission strategy was:

1. compress the main proposal;
2. standardize claim language;
3. center COVID/Kunst and construct-estimate validation;
4. move schema, technical architecture, validation details, HSPC, timeline, and terminology into annexes.

That means annexes are part of the intellectual history, not administrative attachments.

# Open Caveats

- This review did not compare annexes against all older full-example and architecture files.
- Annex E contains a timeline tension with the final rewrite main text; it should remain flagged until a later chronology review.
- Annex D records HSPC reasoning but is not proof of an actual HSPC determination letter.
- Annex B is target architecture, not live runtime evidence.
